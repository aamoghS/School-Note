Totalitarianism - A Permanent Form

Totalitarianism is a permanent form of government, seen as an everlasting emergency.

New Totalitarianism

New totalitarianism is a permanent form of government, also considered as an everlasting emergency.

Right-Wing Totalitarianism Appeared as Fascism

Right-wing totalitarianism appeared in the form of fascism.

Mussolini and Fascist Seizure of Power

Russia, Turkey, and China were considered "backward" countries that were not moving towards democracy.

Italy had parliamentary liberalism starting in 1861.

Mussolini took control in 1921 and created fascism. He was the son of a blacksmith and had a fiery character. Initially, he identified as a left-wing socialist.

He was influenced by the works of Marx, Sorel, and Nietzsche. During the war, he shifted towards nationalism and expressed a desire for conquest, including Austria.

Mussolini and Fascism

1919: Formation of Fascio di Combattimento

In 1919, Mussolini organized a "fascio di combattimento," which translates to a fighting band. The term "fascio" means a bunch of sticks, and Mussolini often evoked ancient glories.

Unrecognized Sacrifices

Italy lost 600,000 lives during the war, and their sacrifices would not be recognized. Wilson rejected the secret treaty, and Britain and France did not support Italy.

Territorial Gains

Italy was only given some Austrian territory as a result of the post-war negotiations.

Wartime Debt and Postwar Depression

Italy suffered from wartime debt and went into a postwar depression.

Rise of Communism

Socialism turned to communism in Italy, and it joined the Third International.

Blackshirts and the Fight Against Communism

The Blackshirts, or Fascists, initially focused on combating communism.

1921: Fascist Electoral Campaign

In 1921, only 35 out of 500 candidates won seats in the elections. The Fascists called for a high levy on capital and profits.

The Fascist State

Mussolini's Battle Against Dissolving Victory and Nation

Mussolini did not sacrifice impunity and began a battle "against the forces dissolving victory and nation."

The Comfort of the Propertied Class

The propertied class found comfort in the fascist movement, where the Blackshirts would beat those who did not support them, all under the banner of law.

1922: March on Rome

In 1922, the "March on Rome" took place, where Blackshirts initiated a coup while Mussolini was in Milan. Mussolini was named premier in a legal sense, and the cabinet resigned.

1924: Fascist Gains Through Intimidation

In 1924, the Fascists gained control of votes through intimidation tactics. Socialist Matteotti exposed the Fascists and died, initially denied by Mussolini, who later admitted involvement during his consolidation of power.

The Fascist State

Under his dictatorship, Mussolini abolished all parties and imposed censorship.

Fascism as an Innovation

Fascism in 1920 was an innovation that showed the world was slow to understand. Mussolini denounced democracy and marginalized minority parties.

Mussolini's Leadership

Mussolini took the title of "leader" or "duce" and denounced other ideas as evil offspring of liberal ideas. He pursued solidarity and made the claim that the "train runs on time" due to his reign.

Syndicalism and the Catholic Church

Left-wing syndicalism looked to revolutionary labor unions that would assume political and economic life. Conservative syndicalism was endured by the Catholic Church.

1929: Mussolini's Peace with the Vatican

Mussolini made peace in 1929 when Vatican City was declared independent.

The Fascist System

The fascist system went through many stages and had 22 major areas known as "corporations," representing groups in the government.

1938: Suspension of the Chamber of Deputies

In 1938, the Chamber of Deputies was suspended by the fascists, while the Senate would exist in its old form. Fascists claimed it was an improvement on 'democracy,' with the Duce having the final say.

Fascist Economic Model

A private enterprise and capitalist economy were the fascist answers to Western-style democracy.

Mussolini and Fascism

Mussolini's Definition of Fascism

Mussolini defined fascism as the "dictatorship of the state over many classes cooperating."

Fascist Response to the Great Depression

When the Great Depression hit Italy, Mussolini blamed the already existing economic problems on it and implemented programs to increase self-sufficiency.

The "Battle of Wheat"

The "Battle of Wheat" initiative allowed for increased food production and the development of hydroelectric power.

Social Structure Under Fascism

Under the fascist regime, there was little change in the structure of society.

Militarization and Imperialistic Ambitions

There was a psychological exhalation which allowed Mussolini to use military and imperialistic power.

Public Perception of Fascism

All ideologies hated fascism, but wealthy people often preferred it.

Totalitarianism: Germany's Third Reich

Rise of Adolf Hitler

Hitler developed a strong dislike for what he saw in Vienna due to the assimilation of German culture. As a young man, he considered himself a pure German and became anti-Semitic. When he moved to Munich, he drifted between jobs and joined the military when the war broke out.

The Rise of Adolf Hitler

Similar to Mussolini

Similar to Mussolini, the excitement of war was thrilling, but when it ended, there was a sense of letdown.

The Birth of the Nazi Party

In Bavaria, a party of old army officers were part of the German Workers' Party. Hitler became a leader and presented a 25-point program. The party was later renamed the National Socialist German Workers Party.

The Brownshirts and Storm Troopers

The Brownshirts, also known as Storm Troopers, were an "army" that was part of the Nazi Party.

The Occupation of the Ruhr

In 1923, the French Army occupied the Ruhr due to unpaid reparations, which prompted the Nazis to attempt to seize power with the "Beer Hall Putsch."

Hitler's Imprisonment and Mein Kampf

Police suppressed the disturbance, and Hitler was initially sentenced to 5 years in jail but was released in less than a year. During his imprisonment, he wrote "Mein Kampf," in which he discussed his racist ideology. The book sold widely and made him a public figure.

The Great Depression and Hitler's Rise

When the Great Depression hit Germany, Hitler transformed the situation to his advantage. German factories came to a halt, resulting in 6 million unemployed. The previous inflation in 1923 had already eroded faith in the economic system.

The Nazi State

Hitler's Use of Propaganda

Hitler used propaganda to denounce the Treaty of Versailles, Weimar democracy, and argued that Germans could only rely on themselves.

Denouncement of Religious Groups

He also denounced Jewish and other religious groups. The left saw Jewish capitalists as anathema, while the right saw Jewish revolutionaries as a horror.

Rise of the Nazi Party

In 1930, the Nazi Party gained 107 seats, compared to the 12 seats they had in 1928. While they initially doubled their votes, they started to lose some support in late 1932.

Support from Nationalist Party and Hindenburg

Hitler received support from other parties that sent funds, notably the Nationalist Party. In 1933, Hindenburg named Hitler Chancellor of the Republic.

Consolidation of Power

To consolidate power, the Nazis set fire to the Reichstag during the voting process and blamed it on communists, allowing Hitler to declare a national emergency.

The Nazi State

Hitler's Rule as the Third Reich

Hitler called his rule the Third Reich, with the first Reich being the Holy Roman Empire and the second being Bismarck's leadership.

Hitler's Title as Leader (Fuhrer)

Hitler took the title of Leader (Fuhrer), and he considered Jewish citizens as un-German.

Nazi Germany and Totalitarianism

The Nuremberg Laws of 1935

The Nuremberg Laws of 1935 deprived Jewish people of their rights.

Kristallnacht: The "Night of Long Knives"

Kristallnacht, also known as the "Night of Long Knives," was an anti-Semitic attack that occurred when a Polish-Jewish student, under pressure from parents, shot a German diplomat. This led to violent actions against Jewish neighborhoods, with the Gestapo implementing harsh measures. Around 30,000 Jewish people were sent to concentration camps, and they were fined one million marks if found guilty.

Restricted Escape for Jews

Doors in Europe and the United States were closed to Jewish people trying to escape.

Internal Conflict in the Nazi Party

While the new totalitarian order was solidified, the Nazi Party experienced internal conflicts, leading to the execution of those who opposed Hitler.

Gestapo, Concentration Camps, and Suppression

The Gestapo and concentration camps were established to suppress individuals considered "un-German."

Government-Supported Pagan Movements

The government encouraged anti-Christian pagan movements.

Replacement of Labor Unions

Labor unions were replaced by the National Labor Front, and strikes were forbidden.

Economic Changes in Nazi Germany

In Nazi Germany, national income was reduced, but people had jobs and more free time. In 1926, the Four Year Plan was introduced to achieve self-sufficiency from foreign trade. They produced substitute products domestically instead of relying on imports.

Industrialization in Nazi Germany

The continent was dependent on international trade, and the Nazi solution was to increase German industrialization to reduce this dependency.
