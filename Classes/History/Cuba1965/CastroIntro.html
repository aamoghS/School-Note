Cuban History and U.S. Involvement

Cuba was once a U.S. protectorate and gained dominance on the island after the Spanish-American War. Prior to the occupation, the USA was a trading partner and invested $30 million in the Cuban economy, leading to their intervention during the Cuban rebellion that eventually resulted in a war.

The U.S. Senate initially opposed the idea of American occupation in Cuba but changed their stance after the war, authorizing a three-year military occupation. Cuba achieved independence in 1902, but the Platt Amendment, introduced by Orville Platt of Connecticut, granted the U.S. the right of military intervention under specific circumstances such as civil war, uncleanliness, and dangerous diseases in Cuba.

The Platt Amendment also imposed restrictions on the capacity of the Cuban government to incur debt and engage in treaties with third powers, while allowing the U.S. to establish naval bases on the island, strategically securing Panama.

The close economic association between the USA and Cuba was evident as the USA had investments in sugar mills, giving U.S. companies 60% of Cuban sugar production by the 1920s. American companies also held large landholdings beyond sugar, including Cuban subsidiaries in electricity, tobacco, and other utilities.

Benefits for Cuba emerged as the U.S. raised the standard of living by 1925, reaching half of the USA standard. Military doctors aided in conquering yellow fever, a scourge for many generations. The USA also built a railway along the length of the island.

This close association also meant that the USA would use Cuba as a scapegoat. U.S. corruption in elections was blamed on Cuba, and the behavior was seen as irritating. Many Americans went to Cuba thinking they were bringing prosperity and tax evasion. A large Cuban middle class became disillusioned, consisting of people who spent years in exile when preaching nationalism.

Cuban nationalism took an anti-American turn as they claimed the U.S. had cheated them of victory, and each new incident of intervention created new waves of resentment. At the University of Havana, students were told that national regeneration was thwarted by "dollar diplomacy."

The anti-American sentiment of Cuban nationalists persisted through 1950, although the Platt Amendment was dead, and U.S. domination was less notable. Nationalists tended to dwell on past wrongs, and Welles' treatment of Martin was still notable. Martin was corrupt but represented the revolution, and it seemed the USA betrayed Cuba.
