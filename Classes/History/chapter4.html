Result of WWI

At the culmination of World War I, when the victors convened in Versailles, the European landscape was in turmoil.
The mighty empires that once held sway had crumbled, paving the way for nascent states eager to claim their mark. Concurrently, the Communist revolution, initially ignited in Russia, had begun to find resonance in other European territories.
Europe, in this post-war aftermath, was grappling with a trinity of crises: the omnipresent specter of starvation, a significant population displaced from their homes, and the devastating blow dealt by the Spanish flu.

Historians remain divided on the effects of the Treaty of Versailles. Some opine that it sowed the seeds for future conflicts, while others argue that those drafting the treaty were out of their depth, failing to fully grasp the complexities and implications of the challenges Europe faced.

Bold Impact of War on Europe

Death Toll

The ravages of the war were unparalleled in history. A staggering 9 million soldiers, which equates to approximately 15 percent of the total forces, paid the ultimate price. The aftermath of the war wasn't just confined to those who perished. Millions more were left physically and mentally scarred, their lives altered irrevocably.

France, in particular, bore a significant portion of this tragedy. They lost a fifth of their soldiers, leading to the grim moniker of the 'lost generation' for those many souls who never returned home.

Yet, it wasn't just soldiers who faced the brunt of the war. Civilians, too, found themselves in the crosshairs, and the situation was further exacerbated by the Spanish flu pandemic, claiming around 20 million lives.

Economic Consequences

The aftermath of the war had profound economic implications:

  • The war dealt a significant financial blow to Britain, costing them a staggering 34 billion.
  • Almost all participating nations had to borrow money to finance the war efforts.
  • The USA, recognizing the dire need, lent out a substantial sum of $2,000 million to both Britain and France.
  • U-Boats had a severe impact on British trade, sinking an alarming 40 percent of British merchant ships.

Physical Impact on Economy

The war also left a trail of physical destruction, further exacerbating the economic turmoil:

  • France bore the brunt of this devastation, with approximately 2 million hectares of its land being rendered unusable.
  • Manufacturing faced a downturn as the immediate need to rebuild homes took precedence.

Political Consequences

Britain

Contrary to many European nations, Britain's political fabric remained relatively stable, with no major upheavals post-war.

Germany

Germany's political landscape underwent significant changes:

  • A socialists-led uprising swept the nation.
  • The Kaiser abdicated in the wake of these upheavals.
  • Friedrich Ebert ascended to leadership, steering Germany through this tumultuous phase.

Russia

Russia witnessed two major revolutions:

  • The first led to the establishment of a provisional government.
  • The subsequent revolution enabled the Bolsheviks to seize power, leading to a string of dictatorships in its wake.

The Habsburg Empire

  • The once-mighty Habsburg Empire disintegrated post-war.
  • Following the Kaiser's abdication, the empire was bifurcated into two states.
  • Austria and Hungary, spurred by nationalistic sentiments, declared their independence, marking the end of centuries-old dominion.

Turkey

The political scene in Turkey underwent a sea change:

  • The sultanate collapsed in 1922.
  • Mustafa Kemal Atatürk rose to power, instituting an authoritarian regime that would shape Turkey's trajectory for decades to come.

The Impact of War Outside of Europe

America

  • The USA emerged from the war as the dominant global economy.
  • The USA took over many European markets, aided by its rapid mechanization.

President Wilson envisioned a greater role for America post-war, but this sentiment was not widely shared among the American populace.

Japan and China

Japan experienced economic prosperity due to its involvement in WWI, finding new markets and avenues for growth.

China sided with the Allies, aiming to exert political and economic influence over Shandong, but these ambitions were thwarted.

Problems with Peacemakers

The Versailles Peace Conference was predominantly influenced by the victors of the war.

  • David Lloyd George - UK
  • George Clemenceau - France
  • Woodrow Wilson - USA
  • Japan focused its energies on the Pacific
  • Vittorio Orlando - Italy, concentrated on territorial acquisitions

The prevailing instability in Europe necessitated a swift peace settlement, further complicating the process.

Aims of the Peacemakers

Woodrow Wilson, with his 14 points, was a beacon of idealism, advocating for a harmonious world. He was firm in his belief that Germany should face repercussions for its actions. His points included:

  • Abolition of covert diplomacy
  • Free maritime navigation for all nations, both during wartime and in peace
  • Promotion of free trade between nations
  • Universal disarmament
  • Colonial populations to have a voice in determining their destinies
  • Withdrawal of German forces from Russia
  • Reinstating Belgium's independence
  • Restoration of Alsace and Lorraine to France
  • Realignment of the Austria-Italy border to better reflect nationalities
  • Upholding the principle of self-determination for the peoples of Austria-Hungary
  • Ensuring Serbia's access to the sea
  • Self-determination rights for the denizens of the Turkish Empire and the unobstructed use of the Dardanelles
  • Elevation of Poland to an independent state, ensuring its sea access
  • Establishment of the League of Nations to safeguard global peace

World Leaders' Views Post-War

The USA was envisaged as the torchbearer of a new world order, promoting the tenets of democracy and the principle of self-determination.

Clemenceau of France advocated for a stringent settlement against Germany, comprising severe economic and territorial sanctions. The underlying objective was to debilitate Germany and ensure it compensated for the losses inflicted during the war.

David Lloyd George of the UK, while advocating for Germany to relinquish its naval supremacy, hoped for the nation's swift economic recovery and stabilization.

Japan and Italy were inclined towards capitalizing on their wartime advancements. Japan harbored aspirations for global racial equality.

Armistice Settlement and the Mood Among the German Population

With the signing of the Armistice, German territories witnessed an occupation by the triumphant Allied forces. This occupation, coupled with General Paul von Hindenburg's sentiments about being "stabbed in the back", instigated the German populace to pinpoint 'internal' adversaries as the culprits for their wartime defeat.

Popular Sentiment Among the "Victors"

Clemenceau and George, influenced by the popular clamor, were nudged towards endorsing a more punitive peace settlement against Germany. Vittorio Orlando of Italy faced domestic pressure to annex more territories in Italy's favor.

In the USA, despite President Woodrow Wilson's efforts, the Treaty of Versailles didn't garner the requisite support in the Republican-dominated House and Senate, resulting in its non-ratification.

Treaty of Versailles

The Treaty was predominantly perceived as a "diktat" since none of the vanquished powers were accorded a say in its formulation.

War Guilt

Germany was mandated to accept the onus of the war, shouldering complete responsibility for its outbreak and ensuing devastation.

Disarmament

While the Treaty laid down provisions for disarmament, Germany's military might was capped, limiting its army to a mere 100,000 men. The French had intentions of annexing the Rhineland, but this culminated in the demilitarization of the region instead.

Territorial Changes

  • Alsace-Lorraine - returned to France
  • Saarland - placed under international administration for 15 years, followed by a plebiscite
  • Parts of Belgium were decided by a plebiscite
  • Germany was partitioned, and Danzig was designated as a free city under international mandate
  • Former Russian territories were transformed into new states

Mandates

The mandates system necessitated Germany to relinquish its colonies to Allied powers, categorized under “A”, “B”, and “C” sectors.

Reparations

The Allies demanded reparations from Germany, approximated to be around 6,600 million pounds.

Punishment of War Criminals

The Treaty of Versailles endorsed the prosecution of Kaiser Wilhelm II. However, the Dutch refused to extradite him.

Criticisms of the Treaty of Versailles

Issue of War Guilt

The “war guilt” clause was met with vehement opposition from Germany, as they believed multiple nations shared the culpability for the war. This very clause was manipulated by Adolf Hitler, channeling the prevailing resentment to consolidate his political ascendancy.

Disarmament Clause

Germany's military was curtailed to a strength of 100,000, while other nations did not face such stringent disarmament obligations.

Reparations and Loss of Key Resources

Germany was burdened with exorbitant reparations and was also compelled to forfeit essential resources. This financial strain pushed Germany into the throes of hyperinflation in the early 1920s.

Issues of Territorial Changes and Self Determination

The territorial realignment resulted in many Germans living outside the redefined German borders, an offshoot of the dissolution of the German Empire.

Relinquishing of Colonies

Under the pretext of liberating territories from Germany's “harsh rule”, its colonies were allocated to other nations.

League of Nations

Germany was not extended an invitation to participate in the League of Nations' meetings, a significant oversight given the nation's prominence.

Failure of Collective Security

In the 1920s and 1930s, the League faced many challenges, but the lack of collective action among European nations led to the failure of the League.

Collective Security and League of Nations

The principle of collective security was the idea that peace could be preserved by countries preventing aggression.

If the League couldn't find a resolution, a group would impose moral pressure and economic sanctions.

The Covenant of the League of Nations

The key objective was to achieve peace and avoid future conflicts.

Aimed at promoting international goodwill and cooperation among 32 allied states and 12 neutral states.

USSR was never admitted, and USA never joined.

Dealing with International Disputes

They had a Permanent Court of International Justice, arbitration, and investigation by the council.

If a member failed to follow, then the league would impose economic sanctions.

The military was the last resort, but the league did not have its own armed forces.
